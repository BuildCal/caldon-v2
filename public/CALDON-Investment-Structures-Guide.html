Introduction

Overview

Why Structure Matters

The legal structure you choose for a property development can significantly impact your tax position, asset protection, liability exposure, and exit flexibility. Getting it right from day one is essential—restructuring mid-project is often costly or impossible.

This guide examines the six most common structures used in Australian property development, exploring when each is appropriate, the advantages and disadvantages, and strategic approaches to maximise returns within the law.

Key Considerations

Tax Efficiency

Different structures attract different tax treatments. Income tax, capital gains tax, GST, and land tax all vary depending on how the investment is held.

Asset Protection

Separating development assets from personal wealth protects against claims arising from the project and limits exposure to project-specific risks.

Liability Management

Development carries inherent risks. The right structure ensures personal assets aren't exposed to project liabilities, builder disputes, or financier claims.

Exit Flexibility

How you intend to exit—sell, hold, refinance, or transfer—should inform your structure choice. Some structures offer more flexibility than others.

Special Purpose Vehicle

Structure 1

Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)

A Special Purpose Vehicle is a company established for a single, specific project. Once the project is complete, the SPV can be wound up, sold, or retained—providing maximum flexibility and clean separation from other business activities.

How It Works

A new proprietary limited company (Pty Ltd) is registered specifically for the development. The SPV holds the land, enters contracts, secures finance, and ultimately owns the completed asset. Shareholders are typically the investor(s) or their related entities.

The SPV operates independently—its own bank accounts, contracts, and liabilities. This "ring-fencing" means project risks don't flow through to other assets or entities.

Who Should Use It

SPVs are ideal for investors undertaking a single project, joint ventures between unrelated parties, or those who want a clean exit strategy. They're particularly useful when the project may be sold as a going concern (selling the company rather than the asset).

Developers with multiple projects often use separate SPVs for each, ensuring one project's issues don't contaminate others.

Advantages

  • Complete liability separation
  • Clean exit—sell company or assets
  • Simple joint venture structure
  • Ring-fenced project risks
  • Can be wound up post-completion
  • Clear ownership and profit allocation

Disadvantages

  • No CGT discount (company rate applies)
  • Setup and ongoing compliance costs
  • Profits taxed at company rate (25-30%)
  • Division 7A issues if loans to shareholders
  • Annual ASIC fees and reporting
  • Potential double taxation on distribution

Strategic Approach: The "Sell the Company" Exit

Rather than selling the completed property (triggering GST and potentially stamp duty for the buyer), consider selling the SPV shares. The buyer acquires the company—and with it, the property. This can defer or reduce tax implications and may be attractive to buyers seeking to acquire the asset's tax history. Structure the shareholding from day one with this exit in mind.

Example Scenario

Two unrelated investors want to develop a site together, contributing 60/40 capital. They establish an SPV with shareholdings reflecting their contributions. All profits and proceeds flow proportionally. On completion, they sell the SPV to a third party wanting a turnkey investment property—avoiding GST on the sale and providing the buyer with existing depreciation schedules.

Company Structure

Structure 2

Company (Pty Ltd)

A standard proprietary limited company used for ongoing development activities rather than a single project. Suitable for developers building a portfolio or conducting multiple projects over time, with profits retained for reinvestment.

How It Works

The company is established as an ongoing trading entity. It can hold multiple properties, conduct successive developments, and retain profits for future projects. Shareholders receive returns through dividends (franked, carrying tax credits) or through eventual sale of shares.

Companies are taxed at a flat rate (25% for base rate entities, 30% otherwise) on profits, regardless of the shareholders' personal tax positions.

Who Should Use It

Companies suit developers intending to conduct multiple projects, reinvest profits rather than distribute them, or build a long-term development business. They're also appropriate where asset protection is paramount and personal liability must be minimised.

Not ideal for one-off projects where the investor wants to access profits personally with CGT discount benefits.

Advantages

  • Flat tax rate (25-30%)
  • Limited liability protection
  • Retained earnings for reinvestment
  • Franked dividends carry tax credits
  • Perpetual existence
  • Well-understood by lenders

Disadvantages

  • No 50% CGT discount
  • Division 7A loan restrictions
  • Potential double taxation
  • Less flexibility than trusts
  • Ongoing compliance requirements
  • Harder to distribute losses

Strategic Approach: The Bucket Company

Use a company as a "bucket" to receive distributions from a family trust, capping tax at the company rate rather than personal marginal rates. The company retains profits for future development funding. When shareholders are in lower tax brackets (retirement, for example), pay franked dividends—the franking credits can result in tax refunds. This approach separates development profits from personal income streams.

Example Scenario

A developer completes 2-3 projects per year and wants to build capital for larger future developments. Rather than distributing all profits (and paying top marginal tax rates), profits are retained in the company at 25% tax. Over five years, retained earnings fund a major project without external finance. The company has also built a track record attractive to future lenders and JV partners.

Division 7A Warning: Loans from a company to shareholders or associates must comply with Division 7A rules or be treated as unfranked dividends. Ensure any drawings are structured as compliant loans with minimum interest rates and repayment terms, or as properly declared dividends.

Discretionary Trust

Structure 3

Discretionary (Family) Trust

A discretionary trust provides flexibility to distribute income and capital gains to beneficiaries in the most tax-effective manner each year. The trustee has discretion over distributions, making it ideal for family wealth structures and estate planning.

How It Works

A trust is established with a trustee (individual or company) who holds assets for the benefit of nominated beneficiaries. Each year, the trustee decides how to distribute income and capital among beneficiaries—typically directing income to those in lower tax brackets.

The trust itself doesn't pay tax; income is taxed in the hands of beneficiaries at their marginal rates. The 50% CGT discount flows through to individual beneficiaries.

Who Should Use It

Discretionary trusts suit families with members in varying tax positions—a high-income earner can direct development profits to a spouse or adult children in lower brackets. They're excellent for asset protection (assets held by the trust, not individuals) and intergenerational wealth transfer.

Less suitable for joint ventures with unrelated parties who need fixed entitlements.

Advantages

  • Flexible income distribution
  • 50% CGT discount available
  • Strong asset protection
  • Estate planning benefits
  • Income splitting among family
  • Land tax threshold in some states

Disadvantages

  • Cannot distribute losses
  • Complex deed requirements
  • Trustee personal liability risk
  • Land tax surcharges may apply
  • Not suitable for unrelated JVs
  • Potential loan restrictions

Strategic Approach: Streaming Capital Gains

When a development property is sold, the capital gain can be "streamed" specifically to beneficiaries who will benefit most from the 50% CGT discount—such as those with capital losses to offset, or lower marginal tax rates. A high-income beneficiary might receive only a nominal distribution while the gain flows to family members in lower brackets, legally minimising the overall tax burden.

Strategic Approach: Corporate Trustee Protection

Appoint a $2 company as trustee rather than an individual. If something goes wrong, the trustee's liability is limited to the company's nominal assets. This provides an additional layer of protection—the individual controllers remain directors but aren't personally exposed as trustee. Ensure the company has no other assets or activities.

Example Scenario

A family trust develops a townhouse project with $400,000 profit. The high-income father (45% bracket) takes a $10,000 distribution. Adult children studying at university (0-19% bracket) each receive $150,000, and the stay-at-home mother ($45,000 other income) receives $90,000. Total family tax: approximately $65,000. If held personally by the father: approximately $180,000.

Unit Trust

Structure 4

Unit Trust

A unit trust provides fixed entitlements based on unit holdings, making it ideal for joint ventures between unrelated parties who need certainty about their share of profits and assets. Each unit holder knows exactly what they're entitled to.

How It Works

The trust issues units to investors in proportion to their capital contributions. A 60/40 joint venture would see one party hold 60 units and the other 40. Income and capital distributions flow according to unit holdings—there's no trustee discretion.

Individual unit holders can access the 50% CGT discount on their share of capital gains. Units can be transferred or sold, providing liquidity options.

Who Should Use It

Unit trusts are the go-to structure for joint ventures between unrelated parties, syndicated investments, or any arrangement requiring fixed, defined profit shares. They provide the certainty that discretionary trusts cannot.

Also useful where unit holders may change during the project—units can be transferred without restructuring the underlying asset ownership.

Advantages

  • Fixed, certain entitlements
  • 50% CGT discount (individuals)
  • Units can be transferred/sold
  • Clear JV profit allocation
  • Familiar to commercial parties
  • Flexible unit classes possible

Disadvantages

  • No income streaming flexibility
  • Fixed allocations lock in tax outcomes
  • Land tax—no threshold (NSW)
  • Stamp duty on unit transfers
  • Cannot distribute losses
  • More complex than company JVs

Strategic Approach: Different Unit Classes

Issue different classes of units with different rights. "A Class" units might receive a priority return (say, 10% p.a.) before "B Class" units share in remaining profits. This allows one investor to take lower risk/lower return while another takes higher risk/higher return. The structure can replicate debt-like returns for conservative investors alongside equity returns for active developers.

Strategic Approach: Discretionary Trust as Unit Holder

Rather than holding units personally, hold them through a discretionary family trust. The unit trust provides fixed entitlements between JV parties, while each party's family trust then has flexibility to distribute their share among family beneficiaries. This combines the certainty of unit trusts with the tax flexibility of discretionary trusts.

Example Scenario

Three investors form a unit trust—Investor A (developer) holds 20 units, Investor B holds 50 units, Investor C holds 30 units. Investors B and C provide capital; A provides expertise and project management. Profits distribute 20/50/30. Each investor's units are held by their respective family trusts, allowing each family to optimise their own tax position while maintaining clear JV entitlements.

Self-Managed Super Fund

Structure 5

Self-Managed Super Fund (SMSF)

Property development within an SMSF offers concessional tax rates but comes with significant restrictions and compliance requirements. When done correctly, it can be a powerful wealth-building strategy for retirement.

How It Works

The SMSF acquires property (potentially using a Limited Recourse Borrowing Arrangement) and either develops or holds it. Rental income is taxed at 15%, capital gains at 10% (if held 12+ months), and assets in pension phase are tax-free.

Strict rules apply: the property cannot be lived in or rented by members or related parties. All decisions must satisfy the "sole purpose test"—providing retirement benefits to members.

Who Should Use It

SMSFs suit investors with substantial superannuation balances seeking long-term property exposure, typically buy-and-hold strategies rather than active development. The concessional tax rates compound significantly over decades.

Less suitable for active development (construction restrictions apply to LRBAs) or those needing access to funds before retirement.

Advantages

  • 15% income tax rate
  • 10% CGT rate (12+ months)
  • Tax-free in pension phase
  • Asset protection from creditors
  • Forced savings discipline
  • Compounds over long term

Disadvantages

  • Cannot access until retirement
  • LRBA restrictions on construction
  • Strict compliance requirements
  • Related party transaction limits
  • Sole purpose test constraints
  • Contribution caps limit capital

LRBA Construction Warning: If using a Limited Recourse Borrowing Arrangement, the property must be a "single acquirable asset" at purchase. Significant construction or development that changes the asset's fundamental nature can breach LRBA rules. Purpose-built development using LRBA debt is generally not permitted. Seek specialist SMSF advice.

Strategic Approach: SMSF as Unit Holder

Rather than the SMSF directly developing property, the SMSF can hold units in a unit trust that undertakes the development. The unit trust (with appropriate structuring) conducts the development while the SMSF simply holds a passive investment. Returns flow to the SMSF at concessional rates. This approach requires careful structuring—ensure SMSF-specific advice is obtained.

Strategic Approach: Pension Phase Development Profits

Time property sales for when members are in pension phase (retirement). A property purchased and developed while in accumulation phase can be sold tax-free once the member retires and commences a pension. The capital gain that would have been taxed at 10% becomes completely tax-free. Requires long-term planning and adequate retirement timeline.

Example Scenario

An SMSF with $800,000 purchases a property for $600,000 (no LRBA). The property is a house on a large block. The SMSF subdivides (minor works, no construction) and sells the rear lot for $350,000, retaining the house. The subdivision profit, taxed at 15%, funds further investments. Had this been done personally at a 45% marginal rate, tax would have been three times higher.

Hybrid Structures

Structure 6

Hybrid Structures

Sophisticated investors often combine multiple structures to optimise for tax efficiency, asset protection, and flexibility simultaneously. These hybrid approaches leverage the advantages of each component structure while mitigating their individual weaknesses.

Common Hybrid Combinations

Unit Trust with Corporate Trustee

A unit trust managed by a company trustee. Combines the fixed entitlements and CGT discount of unit trusts with the limited liability protection of a corporate trustee. The trustee company has no assets beyond its role as trustee, protecting directors from personal liability.

Best For

Joint ventures needing trustee liability protection and fixed profit shares.

Key Benefit

Trustee liability limited to corporate assets while unit holders retain CGT discount.

SPV Held by Family Trust

A special purpose vehicle (company) with shares held by a discretionary family trust rather than individuals. The SPV conducts the development with limited liability, while profits ultimately flow to the trust for flexible distribution among family beneficiaries.

Best For

Single projects where family tax flexibility is desired alongside project ring-fencing.

Key Benefit

Project liability contained in SPV; trust provides distribution flexibility.

Unit Trust with Discretionary Trust Unit Holders

A unit trust where each unit holder is itself a discretionary family trust. The unit trust governs the relationship between JV parties (fixed entitlements), while each party's trust manages their family's tax position (flexible distribution).

Best For

Joint ventures between families wanting both certainty and tax flexibility.

Key Benefit

Clear JV entitlements; each family optimises their own tax independently.

Strategic Approach: The "Development Company + Holding Trust" Split

Use a company to conduct development activities (trading income taxed at company rate, liability contained), but have a discretionary trust hold completed investment properties (rental income distributed flexibly, CGT discount on eventual sale). Development profits retained in the company fund the next project; stabilised assets transfer to the trust for long-term family wealth.

Advanced Strategies

Maximising Returns

Strategic Structuring Approaches

Beyond choosing the right entity, sophisticated structuring can significantly enhance after-tax returns. These strategies are used by experienced developers and high-net-worth investors—always with appropriate professional advice.

1. The Profit Split Structure

Separate land ownership from development activity. A passive entity (trust) holds the land and receives a "land owner's profit" while an active entity (company) conducts the development and receives a "developer's margin." This can allocate more profit to the structure with better tax characteristics for that income type.

Tax Efficiency Risk Management

2. The Pre-Sale CGT Strategy

For projects held long enough to qualify for CGT discount (12+ months), timing the contract date is critical. Exchange contracts after the 12-month threshold even if settlement occurs later. For trusts, ensure the capital gain is properly "streamed" to beneficiaries who can best utilise the discount.

CGT Planning Timing

3. The Debt Recycling Approach

Use development profits to pay down non-deductible personal debt (home mortgage), then reborrow against the property for the next investment. Over time, all debt becomes deductible investment debt. The structure holding investments should be the borrower, making interest tax-deductible.

Interest Deductions Wealth Building

4. The Family Employment Strategy

Where family members genuinely contribute to the development (administration, project oversight, sales), employ them through the development entity. Wages are tax-deductible to the entity and taxed at the employee's marginal rate—potentially much lower. Must reflect genuine work at market rates.

Income Splitting Deductions

5. The Margin Scheme for GST

When selling residential property, the GST margin scheme calculates GST only on the "margin" (sale price minus original purchase price) rather than the full sale price. This can significantly reduce GST liability. Must be elected correctly and requires specific contract clauses.

GST Minimisation Residential Sales

Professional Advice Essential: These strategies involve complex tax law and must be implemented correctly to be effective. Anti-avoidance provisions can apply to arrangements lacking commercial substance or entered into primarily for tax benefits. Always work with qualified tax advisers and ensure arrangements have genuine commercial rationale beyond tax outcomes.

Summary

Conclusion

Structuring for Success

The right investment structure provides the foundation for a successful development. It determines how profits are taxed, how risks are contained, and how flexibly you can respond to changing circumstances. Getting it wrong can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars over the life of a project.

Key Takeaways

Structure before you commit. Establish the right entity before signing any contracts or paying deposits. Restructuring later triggers stamp duty, CGT, and complexity.

Match structure to strategy. Your exit plan should drive your structure choice. A long-term hold has different needs than a build-and-sell project.

Consider the whole picture. Tax efficiency matters, but so do asset protection, estate planning, financing, and simplicity. Don't over-engineer.

Get professional advice. Every situation is different. The cost of proper advice is negligible compared to the cost of getting it wrong.
